at_glance:
  - The study evaluated the outcome of surgical correction of carpal valgus in New World South American camelids.
  - Procedures that included a distal radial transfixation pin were more often associated with success after a single surgery.
  - A deformity angle greater than 19° and an age younger than 4 months at the time of surgery were associated with a worse outcome.
  - Overall, 74% of limbs were straightened, but in some cases multiple procedures were required.
objective: The aim of the study was to describe the characteristics of the patients and radiographic findings of camelids treated surgically for carpal valgus and to identify factors associated with the outcome of surgical treatment. The authors also wanted to verify whether the inclusion of a distal radial transphyseal bridging improved the surgical outcome.
background: |-
  Carpal valgus is a common angular deformity in New World South American camelids. There is little peer-reviewed information on the effectiveness of surgical treatments in camelids, despite the condition impacting animal welfare and economic value.
  Treatment strategies have already been defined for foals, but indications for crias remain less established. The authors also highlight the possible role of factors such as heredity, trauma, hypophosphatemic rickets, and distal ulnar growth retardation.
methods: |-
  Clinical records and radiographs of camelids surgically treated for carpal valgus over a 13-year period, from 1987 to 2010, were retrieved. Clinical, anamnestic, and radiographic data were collected, including species, sex, age, deformity angle, site of the center of rotation of angulation, and presence of physeal abnormalities.
  Surgical outcome was assessed after the first surgery and after all subsequent corrective procedures. Uni- and multivariate statistical analyses were performed to identify factors associated with treatment success or failure.
protocol:
  patient: 19 New World camelids, for a total of 33 surgically treated limbs; 11 llamas and 8 alpacas.
  anaesthetic: ''
  surgical: Surgical correction of carpal valgus using various combinations of ulnar ostectomy, periosteal transection and elevation, distal radial transphyseal bridging, and, in one case, radial wedge ostectomy.
  nerve_block: The evaluated techniques included distal radial transphyseal bridging, alone or in combination with other growth modulation procedures, to correct the angular deformity. In some cases, ulnar ostectomy was also performed to reduce the tension band effect of the ulna on the distal radius.
  monitoring: Clinical and radiographic follow-up, with evaluation of the absence of valgus by the veterinarian at the time of implant removal or at least 30 days after surgery.
findings:
  - Procedures that included a distal radial transphyseal bridging had a higher probability of success after a single surgery.
  - An initial angulation greater than 19° was associated with a lower probability of success.
  - Crias younger than 4 months at the time of surgery had a less favorable outcome.
  - Overall, 74% of limbs straightened, 15% resulted in overcorrection, and 11% maintained persistent valgus.
  - In some cases, valgus recurred after implant removal.
clinical_implications:
  - Distal radial transphyseal bridging should be considered in the initial surgery of more severe cases.
  - In very young crias, early surgery may not be the best choice if the deformity is not rapidly worsening.
  - Cases with marked angulation are more likely to require more than one procedure.
  - Recurrence after implant removal suggests the need for careful follow-up.
limitations:
  - This is a retrospective study, with intrinsic limitations in selection and data quality.
  - Not all animals had complete follow-up.
  - Surgical protocols were not uniform across cases.
  - Data on nutritional and metabolic status, including vitamin D, were incomplete.
take_home:
  - In camelids with carpal valgus, distal radial transphyseal bridging increases the likelihood of success.
  - Greater angulation and very young age can worsen the surgical prognosis.
  - Some cases require multiple procedures to achieve satisfactory correction.
  - Follow-up after implant removal is essential to identify any recurrences.

This study provides a useful and practical contribution to the surgical management of carpal valgus in South American camelids, a clinically relevant but poorly standardized condition in the literature. The main strength is the retrospective analysis of a relatively large number of cases, with evaluation of prognostic factors that can guide therapeutic decisions. In particular, the association between distal radial transphyseal bridging and favorable outcome is clinically interesting information for the veterinary surgeon.
The study limitations are typical of a retrospective design: heterogeneity of surgical protocols, follow-up not always complete, and possible uncontrolled confounding variables. Additionally, the presence of cases treated over different periods may reflect an evolution of techniques and clinical experience over time. Overall, the work is useful for guiding surgical practice, but conclusions should be interpreted with caution in the absence of a controlled prospective study.
