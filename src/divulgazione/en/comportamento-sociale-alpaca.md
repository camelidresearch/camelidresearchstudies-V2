Alpacas (_Vicugna pacos_) are herd animals, domesticated in the Andes several thousand years ago. Their social nature is not a minor detail: it is a biological characteristic that has direct consequences on their management and well-being.

## Group animals, not solitary ones

In the wild and in farming, alpacas live in groups and develop social hierarchies. An alpaca kept alone tends to show signs of stress and discomfort: for this reason, it is generally not recommended to raise a single individual. The presence of other camelids provides security and allows the animal to express its natural behavioral repertoire.

## How they communicate

Alpacas communicate through body postures, ear and tail movements, and a range of vocalizations. The most common sound is a soft humming (_humming_), which accompanies various situations: curiosity, discomfort, or contact between mother and offspring. In alarm situations, they emit a sharp and piercing call that warns the entire group of a potential danger.

## "Spitting": a signal, not a whim

The best-known behavior of alpacas — spitting — is actually a form of social communication. It is used mainly among individuals to establish hierarchies or to keep another animal away from food. It is rare for an alpaca accustomed to humans to spit at people: when it happens, it is often a sign of stress or management that does not respect its needs.

## Why knowing these behaviors matters

Recognizing the social language of alpacas helps manage them in a respectful way and to identify early signs of distress. An animal that isolates itself from the group, changes its vocalizations, or shows unusual behaviors may signal a health or well-being issue that deserves attention.

## In summary

Alpacas are social animals that communicate in a refined way through postures, sounds, and behaviors. Understanding their herd nature is the foundation of respectful management focused on their well-being.
